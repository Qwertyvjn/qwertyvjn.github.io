IPCC Carbon Counting — Methodology

1 & 2. The Foundation: What is Carbon Counting and the IPCC?

Carbon Counting is the process of quantifying the total amount of greenhouse gases (CO₂e) emitted directly and indirectly by an individual, organization, or country over a specific period. It's the primary tool for measuring climate impact.

The foundation for this entire process comes from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), which provides the scientific guidelines and consensus used globally. Organizations then translate these guidelines into actionable frameworks, most notably the Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Protocol, which structures how emissions are measured.

3. Why It Matters: Management and Mitigation

Counting carbon is essential because you cannot manage what you do not measure. It identifies emission hotspots and allows engineers to target the most effective areas for reduction.

Examples of Usage:

  • Policy: Governments use national inventories (based on IPCC) to set climate targets.
  • Investment: Companies use carbon data to report to investors.
  • Engineering: Engineers use CO₂e metrics in Life Cycle Assessments (LCA).

5. Scope and Tier Breakdown (Interactive Diagram)

The three Scopes define where the emissions occur. Use the cards below to explore the methodology.

Scope 1: Direct Emissions

Emissions from sources you own or control — fuel combustion, company vehicles, fugitive emissions, etc.

Scope 2: Purchased Energy

Indirect emissions from the generation of purchased electricity, steam, heating, and cooling.

Scope 3: Value Chain

All other indirect emissions in your value chain — usually the largest category (purchased goods, business travel, waste, etc.).

Example Calculation (Scope 2)

100 kWh × 0.71 kg CO₂/kWh = 71 kg CO₂e
