1 & 2. The Foundation: What is Carbon Counting and the IPCC?

Carbon Counting is the process of quantifying the total amount of greenhouse gases (CO2e) emitted directly and indirectly by an individual, organization, or country over a specific period. It's the primary tool for measuring climate impact.

The foundation for this entire process comes from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), which provides the scientific guidelines and consensus used globally. Organizations then translate these guidelines into actionable frameworks, most notably the Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Protocol, which structures how emissions are measured.

3. Why It Matters: Management and Mitigation

Counting carbon is essential because you cannot manage what you do not measure. It identifies emission hotspots and allows engineers to target the most effective areas for reduction.

Examples of Usage:

  • Policy: Governments use national inventories (based on IPCC) to set climate targets and sign international treaties.
  • Investment: Companies use carbon data to report to investors and set corporate sustainability goals (e.g., "Net-Zero by 2050").
  • Engineering: Engineers use CO2e metrics in Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) to design lower-impact products.

4. The Process: How to Do Carbon Counting

The process is based on a simple relationship that determines the emissions for any activity:

CO2e = Activity Data × Emission Factor

Where Activity Data is the amount of resource used (e.g., 100 kWh of electricity), and the Emission Factor is the known CO2e intensity of that resource (e.g., 0.71 kg CO2e per kWh in Indonesia).

Selected Scope Breakdown

Scope 1: Direct Emissions (In My Backyard)

Emissions directly from sources you **own or control**. These are the most measurable emissions.

Examples: Burning fuel in company cars/boilers, manufacturing process emissions, company-owned refrigerants.

Relevant Tier Methodology:

  • Tier 1: Basic Fuel Use (based on national/global average fuel factor).
  • Tier 2: Equipment-Specific Factors (e.g., using a factor specific to your boiler type).
  • Tier 3: Continuous Monitoring (e.g., live measurements of stack emissions).

Scope 2: Energy Consumption (My Utility Bill)

Emissions from the **generation of purchased electricity, steam, heating, and cooling** consumed by your company.

Example: The CO2 released at the power plant that generates the electricity you buy for your office. Calculation can be location or market-based.

Relevant Tier Methodology:

  • Tier 1: Location-Based Average (using the regional grid factor, like the PLN grid factor).
  • Tier 2: Supplier-Specific Data (if your provider has a cleaner, verifiable mix).
  • Tier 3: Hourly/Market-Based Data (reflecting real-time energy purchases or specific renewable energy certificates).

Scope 3: Value Chain (Everything Else)

All other indirect emissions that occur in the **value chain** (both upstream and downstream). This is usually the largest category and requires the use of Tiers to manage data quality.

Examples: Business travel, employee commuting, manufacturing of purchased goods, waste disposal, and end-of-life treatment of sold products.

Relevant Tier Methodology:

  • Tier 1 (Simplest): Uses **industry-average** emission factors (e.g., generic laptop CO2 factor). Quick and cheap.
  • Tier 2 (Better): Uses **regionally specific** data or economic input/output models.
  • Tier 3 (Best): Uses **supplier-specific primary data** (e.g., surveying the exact factory emissions). The gold standard, but requires significant effort.

Example Calculation (Scope 2)

To calculate the emissions from 100 kWh of electricity (Scope 2):

100 kWh × 0.71 kg CO2/kWh = 71 kg CO2e
